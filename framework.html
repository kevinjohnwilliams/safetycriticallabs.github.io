The SCL AI Requirements Framework establishes verifiable, pass/fail requirements for each AI-specific failure mode not addressed by existing spaceflight, aviation, or safety-critical software standards. Algorithm-agnostic. Domain-applicable. Openly published under a citable DOI.

AI-1
Data partitioning & classification
Training/validation/test separation with documented provenance. CUI and ITAR classification inheritance through AI output channels.
Requires explicit documentation of data lineage, version control of training datasets, and formal inheritance of data classification through all model outputs. Addresses the foundational quality control gap in AI system documentation.
All classifications
AI-2
Bias detection & mitigation
Baseline performance across user classes and operational contexts. Bias-free training data requirements with verifiable inspection criteria.
Performance must be characterized across all relevant user populations and environmental conditions. Verification cannot rest on policy commitments alone — inspection criteria must be defined, documented, and testable.
All classifications
AI-3
ML test coverage
A defined test matrix as the ML equivalent of code coverage — spanning nominal performance, edge cases, failure mode injection, and distributional boundary testing.
Existing standards define software test coverage through structural metrics (branch, decision, MC/DC). AI systems require an equivalent test taxonomy spanning behavioral dimensions not captured by code analysis alone.
All classifications
AI-4
Continuous validation
Post-deployment drift detection with defined thresholds, revalidation triggers, and escalation paths. Closes the single largest gap in existing spaceflight and aviation standards.
Both NPR 7150.2D and DO-178C assume V&V occurs at delivery and behavior is stable thereafter. AI systems do not comply with this assumption. Continuous validation requirements are the direct technical response to that structural gap.
All classifications
AI-5
Hallucination prevention
Output confidence bounding with defined thresholds for safety-critical decisions. Graceful degradation to human override below threshold.
Confident, plausible, incorrect outputs are not recognized by any current spaceflight or aviation standard. AI-5 requires bounded confidence outputs, defined safety thresholds, and specified degradation paths to human authority when model confidence falls below acceptable levels.
All classifications
AI-6
Out-of-distribution detection
Detection, logging, and escalation of inputs outside the training distribution — the condition under which AI behavior is least predictable.
No current standard requires OOD detection. AI-6 mandates that systems identify when inputs fall outside their training distribution, log these events with sufficient fidelity for post-mission analysis, and escalate according to a defined protocol.
All classifications
AI-7
Adversarial robustness
Adversarial testing protocols, defined robustness bounds, and specified behavioral requirements under adversarial input conditions.
Safety-critical AI systems must be assessed against adversarial inputs that exploit known vulnerabilities in the model architecture. Robustness bounds must be defined, documented, and verifiable. Required for any system operating in an environment where adversarial manipulation is a plausible threat.
Safety-critical only
AI-8
Explainability
Operator-accessible decision reasoning with traceable decision basis. Required for any AI system where a traditional safety case would demand logical inspection.
Traditional safety cases require traceable logic from requirements to implementation. AI-8 establishes an equivalent requirement: operator-accessible reasoning that allows a qualified engineer to understand the basis for any safety-relevant decision made by the system.
Safety & mission critical
AI-9
Human-AI teaming
Defined AI authority limits, human override requirements, trust calibration testing, and interaction audit trails.
Built from the operational realities of spaceflight crew dynamics. AI-9 requires that AI authority limits be explicitly defined, human override capability be maintained and tested, crew trust calibration be assessed against known AI error rates, and all human-AI interactions be logged for mission review.

Classification levels

Three tiers. Scaled to consequence.

The framework applies all nine requirement areas at different depths depending on the classification of your AI system. Classification is determined during Phase 1 of the assessment.

Tier 1
Safety-Critical
AI systems where failure could result in loss of human life, loss of the vehicle, or catastrophic mission failure with no recovery path. Full requirement set applies. Surveillance audits required annually.

Tier 2
Mission-Critical
AI systems where failure would compromise mission objectives or require significant mission replanning, but with recoverable consequences. Adversarial robustness requirement (AI-7) may be tailored based on threat environment.
AI-1 AI-2 AI-3 AI-4 AI-5 AI-6 AI-8 AI-9
Tier 3
Mission Support
AI systems supporting mission operations where failure has recoverable consequences with operational workarounds available. Explainability (AI-8) and human-AI teaming (AI-9) requirements may be scoped to the specific operational context.

Open standard

A standard anyone
can read and challenge.

The framework is publicly available on GitHub and formally citable with a DOI through Zenodo. We certify against a standard that is fully transparent — not a proprietary black box.

Transparency in the standard is the foundation of trust in any certification authority. The methodology for how we conduct assessments is proprietary. The standard we assess against is not.
